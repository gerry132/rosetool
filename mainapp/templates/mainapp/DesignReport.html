Design Report

Introduction

April Greiman was one of the first designers to embrace computers as a graphic design tool and created iconic images that defined the 80's and 90's, "She has been called a pioneer in this regard, making it acceptable for a graphic designer to explore their craft using a computer." (Smith, 2009). At the time her designs and the methods she used were thought as being radical but it also earned her great acclaim, designer Massimo Vignelli said that she was “by far the most daring and meaningfully experimental graphic designers in the world, her work is extremely intellectual and extremely emotional at the same time.” (Whiteson, 1988)

Early Life

Born in 1948 in New York city April had a creative mind from a young age. Her father was of the earliest computer programmers and was introduced to technology as a child. She attended Kansas City Art Institute an upon graduating decided to further her studies at the prestigious Basel School of Design in Switzerland. She moved to Los Angeles and it was there that she established herself as an ground-breaking artist that changed the landscape of design forever.

Cultural Context

The MAC computer was released in 1984 and with it came software tools that offered graphic capabilities. April Greiman began to use this then new technology to create innovative pieces of art that were never before seen. The Mac then came with a bitmap editor known as MacPaint, a vector based graphics program called MacDraw, and a word-processor called MacWrite. With her training in graphic design from the Basel school in Switzerland and the techniques she picked up from the Swiss style and Weingart's radical New Wave style she used the new technologies introduced by MAC to create her own distinctive style. The first major piece she created was a fold out poster for Design Quarterly titled 'Does it make sense?'. The fold out sent out shock waves in the design community many were aghast at the usage of technology to create a graphic piece to be featured in a well-known magazine.

“The people--mainly other designers--who were threatened by my innovations were the most bitter. But these criticisms, by forcing me to articulate my ideas, only served to sharpen my style.” ("A Designing Woman With Radical Ideas : April Greiman Says Her Graphics Style Is 'an Experiment in Creating "Hybrid Imagery" '" 2020)

The two page spread was 6 feet long and was a life size digital portrait and created using the forementioned programs on the Mac computer and had to print in seperate segments with each segment taking the whole night to complete. She would often send it to print before she left the studio to have it ready for the next day. Her intention behind this piece was to offer an view of what technology was capabile of and as she stated was inspired by the notion by philiosopher Ludwig Wittgenstein “If you give it a sense, it makes sense.” (Ocegueda 2020)

"Using my professional video equipment and new software and hardware from Apple I composed a full-sized portrait of myself and handed off to the printer a stack of dot-matrix printed paper with a low resolution, 512k image. It was both terrifying and exhilarating. Learning something new in technology and exposing myself personally to demonstrate a transmedia idea." ("Neshan 27: Face to Face/ Made in Space; An Interview with April Greiman/ Majid Abbasi" 2020)

Her process was very complex and included a variety of mediums. An example of her process is laid out in the creation of the Modern Poster for the MOMA museum in New York. Firstly she would create a background using video images and transfering the image to the computer using the Mac paintbox tool. Secondly she would add sketches, photographs and textures. Lastly "This “layered” image is laser scanned to magnify the light-point pixels that make up the computer image. Each magnified pixel thus becomes a unique and separate geometry of tones and colors." (WHITESON, 1988). In the finished piece elements of her Swiss Style trainning can be seen such as the use of a grid layout and skewed perspectives.


However by far her greatest influence came form her mentor and teacher Wolfgang Weingart who worked at the Basel school of art.

Style

Much of April Greimans work as been informed by her education at the Basel school of design in Switzerland. Her work contains elements of the Swiss style but most notably it is heavily inspired by her mentor Wolfgang Weingart. Wolfgang Weingart is credited as being the originator of the style, it was intended to break on conventions of design practice and because of its non-conformist leanings it is heavily associated with the punk movement of the 70's. Like punk it was seen as a rejection of modernist values.

As traditional attitudes came to be considered outdated, society rebelled against the mainstream and demanded change. (Hyndman, 2019)

The new wave style is characterized by alternating sizes such as letting of differing size, font, spacing or width. Punk and the new wave movement promoted the ethos of DIY design and this is the main reason that April Greiman embraced new computer technologies as it allowed her to have direct access to typefaces as previously graphic designers needed to commission a typesetter to create the type giving them little visibility in the process.(Hyndman, 2019). Greiman also used various mediums to create her work such as photographs, textures and sketches very much echoing the DIY aesthetic of Punk. Greiman was also a fan of using geometic shapes in her work playing with their perspectives to give the illusion of a 3-Dimensional object in a 2-Dimensional space.

Impact on Graphic Design

There are many contempoary artists that have emulated this style for example Irish designer Aoife Dunne. Like April Greiman she uses computer generated graphics that are reminiscent many of her designs she is quoted as saying "I always saw technology as my paintbrush."

The use of computer graphics and the varied use of colour, pattern and texture is quite reminiscent of April Greimans work. Aoife Dunne creates an interactive sensory experince through her work. Like Greiman she is multidisciplinary but takes Greimans work even further by creating her own websites and animations. She describes her style as post-pop or post-internet which are movements that takes cues from 90's and early 2000's internet culture.

Being very adapt at creating graphics she created an installation called Limitless which is an immersive video game world infused with trippy graphics. In the video there are many elements that are inspired by the new wave style such as varying font sizes and dense patterns.

Conclusion

Advances in technology informed a lot of April Greimans work in the 80's, the introduction of the personal computer and graphic capabilities allowed her to create a whole new style and helped normalise the use of technology in Graphic Design so what does the future hold for graphic design, how will technology chnage?

We are witnessing a great paradigm shift in regards to graphic design in use, we are creating a greater distance with design and traditonal methods mainly print even now graphic design is highly dependent on technology. 3D technology and augmented reality will most certainly be something graphic designers be working with. Augmented reality defined as "a view of the real, physical world in which elements are enhanced by computer-generated input" and this means graphic designers will start to create things that are not confined to a page or screen but be integrated with the users everyday reality.

Its strange to think that not that long ago the thought of using a computer to genreate art was thought as being akin to heresy. For every graphic artist a computer is an essential part of their work and to some it is their work. April Greiman was able to give us a hint at the possibilites technology held for the evolution of design in a sense she open the door.
