Week 4: The International Typographic Style

The International Typographic style or Swiss style as its also known first emerged in the 1940's and gained popularity in the 1950's. The Swiss style owes its name to the place of its origin Switzerland mainly "the “kunstgewerbeschule” in Zurich, led by “Josef Müller-Brockmann”, and the “Allgemeine Gewerbeschule” in Basel, led by “Armin Hofmann”." (ricardorodrigues11 2020)

The Swiss style is characterized by its use of the grid system using simple legible fonts such as san serif fonts such as Helvetica Sans-serif which was created by Max Miedinger and Eduard Hoffmann. The Swiss style promoted the principles of Cleanliness. Readability. Objectivity. The usage of a grid system gives this style the ability to balance all the elements on the page in a harmonious manner. You will notice that many examples of Swiss style lack any sort of illustrations as the designers mostly used photography or even just a typeface. It owes many of its aesthetics and principles to the Bauhaus school which promoted the principles of form follows function and the usage of a grid system.
