Week 1: The Industrial Revolution

The Industrial Revolution marked a period of rapid development in the western world, societies moved from a mostly agrarian structure to an Inustrial one. With these rapid changes came the need for effective communication that could be easilt reproduced and as a result many new typographic methods, styles and technologies emerged during this era.

"The design styles that emerged around the jturn of the century were symptomatic of people trying to cope with and assimilate the new industrialized landscape into their lives" - Rabinowitz

The mechanization of society was mirrored in the world of advertising and publication to meet the demand to mass produce products and materials. Some of the most significant developments saw graphic design moving away from handcrafts. In the field of Typography new typefaces emerged such as Slab Serif, Egyptian, Sans Serif and many more.

The invention of the lithographic printing press offered artists the opportunity to realistically recreate images

With this shift towards the mechanization of design many looked backward to the old techniques and processes and what emerged became known as the Arts and Crafts movement. "The birth of the Arts & Crafts movement in Britian in the late 19th century marked the begining of a change in the value society placed on how things were made. This was a reaction to not only the damaging effects of industrialization but also the relatively low status of the decorative arts"

The most notable and well known propoent of the use of handcrafts over mechanical means was English artist William Morris. William Morris was inspired by the writings of John Ruskin and believed that society should work toward the happiness and wellbeing of the individual. He believed this uptoian ideal could be achieved through the marraige of art and labour. In 1861 William Morris established Morris & Co. to actualise his principles in design. His style can be characterised as being heavily influenced by medieval English aesthetic.
