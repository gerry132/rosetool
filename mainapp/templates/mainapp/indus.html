Week 1: The Industrial Revolution to the Arts & Crafts Movement

The Industrial Revolution marked a period of rapid development in the western world, societies moved from a mostly agrarian structure to an Industrial one. With these rapid changes came the need for effective communication that could be easilt reproduced and as a result many new typographic methods, styles and technologies emerged during this era.

Fig 1: Industrialized city during the 19th century

"The design styles that emerged around the turn of the century were symptomatic of people trying to cope with and assimilate the new industrialized landscape into their lives" (Rabinowitz, 2015)


The mechanization of society was mirrored in the world of advertising and publication to meet the demand to mass produce products and materials. Some of the most significant developments saw graphic design moving away from handcrafts. In the field of Typography new typefaces emerged such as Slab Serif, Egyptian, Sans Serif and many more.


Fig 2: Example of Slab font

The invention of the lithographic printing press also offered artists the opportunity to realistically recreate images. The growth of machines also helped to develop graphic design as a practice and many aspects of visual communication began to become segmented And more speciaised.

Fig 3: 19th century lithographic poster

The emergence of the photography also revolutionized visual communication and began to gain popularity with artists. Firstly discovered by Frenchman Joseph Niepce (1765–1833), it was his experiments that laid the foundations of the practice today.




Fig 4: First photograph created by Niepce



Whilst the Industrial Revolution was in full momentum, the Victorian era which ran from 1937 to 1901 ushered in lavishly styled posters and packaging. Many artists created opulent illustrations with ornate borders and romantisied imagery, lettering in particular was emphasised and the main focal point of the page.




With this shift towards the mechanization of design many looked backward to the old techniques and processes and what emerged became known as the Arts and Crafts movement.

"The birth of the Arts & Crafts movement in Britian in the late 19th century marked the begining of a change in the value society placed on how things were made. This was a reaction to not only the damaging effects of industrialization but also the relatively low status of the decorative arts" (V&A · Arts and Crafts: an introduction, 2020)

The most notable and well known propoent of the use of handcrafts over mechanical means was English artist William Morris. William Morris was inspired by the writings of John Ruskin and believed that society should work toward the happiness and wellbeing of the individual. He believed this uptoian ideal could be achieved through the marraige of art and labour. In 1861 William Morris established Morris & Co. to actualise his principles in design. His style can be characterised as being heavily influenced by medieval English aesthetic.

Graphic Design as a industry owes a lot to the period of the industrial revolution without the strides in technology and the use of tools the refinement of techniques we know today would not have happened. Many companies still choose to design their brand using Victorian aesthetics.
